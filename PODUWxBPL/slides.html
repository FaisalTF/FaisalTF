Introduction

This analysis explores trends throughout the programs provided within the ImBlaze platform, the benefeciary students and their experience, partner schools and mentors, and the industries these opportunities are happening within, with the hopes of accomplishing the following:


  • To help the ImBlaze team maintain and adapt the platform to student needs.

  • More broadly, to understand student experiences and interests towards next steps at the high school level.

Disclaimer Caveats:

    Along with the insights brought up throughout this analysis, the following points need to be taken into account:

  • This analysis explores data from within a specific time-window,* a window that may have been influenced by driving factors (seasonal, educational, that may prevent extrapolating these temporal trends linearly or otherwise constantly.

  • Trends relating to opportunity fields and industries all directly rely on ImBlaze's expansion history and patterns, which occour irregularly across different fields - program addition frequently occours through staff and student connections, which may be skewed throughout the different schools.

Student Activity

The previous chart visualizes student log counts per month. Annual trends in student activity seem to peak during the second half of the year, potentially relevant to summertime and school vacations. The COVID-19 pandemic is also notably represented by the drop off during the early months of 2020. The number of logs drops from approximately 17,500 in February 2020 to around 250 logs in March 2020 which precisely aligns with the start of the pandemic in the United States. However, while health protocols and measures from the pandemic were still enforced during the following year (2021), activity seemed to have risen back surpassing 2019 numbers.

Internship Request and Demand


Student internship requests peaked in 2019 then fell during the pandemic. Internship requests from students grew more than three-fold from 2017 to 2019 but fell in 2020 as the COVID-19 pandemic took hold. Requests in the first half of 2021 are returning to pre-pandemic levels, suggesting that the effect is transitory.

Fewer students are requesting internships for each opening.
The request density (the average number of internship requests for each internship opening) fell from 2.3 in 2017 to 1.6 in 2020.

Industries and Professions

The education, healthcare, and animal care industries have consistently provided the most internship opportunities.
In every year from 2017 to 2020, these three industries opened the most internship opportunities on the ImBlaze platform. Virtually all industries provided fewer opportunities in 2020 than in 2019, but the drop was especially sharp for Education – as widespread school closures may suggest. The number of healthcare opportunities decreased less than most other categories.

Least Met Industries

Student interest in internships is less met in animal care, museum/library, and music professions.
The ratio of applicants to open internship opportunities was highest for the above industries (top 5).

Which Factors Motivate Internship Applicants in ImBlaze?

This subsection explores student application comments inside the ImBlaze app to better understand the motivations of students seeking internships. This analysis points to three key topics of interest and popular fields, which can help ImBlaze better identify opportunities and design experiences tailored to its users’ interests.

Topic Clustering, a Natural Language Processing (NLP) technique, was used to distinguish student applications and identify topic groups. The Latent Dirichlet allocation (LDA) model, more specifically, identified the following topics based on keyword relevance. Larger keywords are identified as being more commonly associated with the topic.

Interest in specific disciplines/fields of work: One of the most common keywords students used was passion, which was closely associated with field and discipline-specific keywords, like medical, cars, or design. Interest in the medical, automotive, and art/design industries were particularly common.

Career Preparation: On the other hand, other applicants were more interested in gaining general work and internship experience, rather than exploring a passion in a specific field. These responses focused on areas like interview(s) and career(s).

Finding interesting experiences: The third cluster of responses is interpreted to be more experience-driven and exploratory. These responses shared motivations towards cool or interesting experiences, with a focus on interesting learning experiences.

Other key topics: The model also identified a fourth cluster. Lacking strong definitive keywords, it identifies other common themes less related to the previous three topics. Notable motivations include helping children and shadow(ing) opportunities

Student Suggestions - Overview

In contrast to activity peaking during the end of the academic year in the summer, suggestions peak following the summer at the start of the academic year. September marks the annual peak in suggestion volume, rising from around 500 suggestions in 2017, to more than 1400 suggestions in September of 2019. A drop-off likely due to the COVID-19 pandmic is yet again present in suggestion trends in 2020.

Student Suggestions - Motivations

Comments accompanying suggestions were analyzed, similar to those accompanying applications in the previous section, with the highlighted keywords helping identify themes and motives behind suggestions.

Similar themes are present, such as exploring personal passions (keywords photography; music), as well as career development (keywords future; experience).

Analyzing Student Sentiments

Sentiment Analysis: To dive deeper into understanding the student experience, another Natural Langauge Processing (NLP) technique was preformed. Sentiment Analysis utilizes machine learning to try to extract emotions and attitudes from a given piece of text, and ranks its sentiment from most positive, to most negative.

In this analysis, sentiment analysis was performed,* ranking each student log entry with a sentiment score ranging from 1 (most positive), to -1 (most negative); with 0 representing neutral sentiment according to the model.

An additional measurment was calculated, Sentiment Difference, which is simply the difference between a student's sentiment scores during their check-in and check-out comments. Iedally, this would capture a student's setniment towards how they felt about their day.

Sentiment difference scores had a mean of -0.014, indicating that check-out logs had slightly more negative setniment in average, compared to logs during check-in.

Student Setniments and Mentor Input: Student logs that were accompanied with mentor comments (n = 4829) had a more positive average sentiment differnece (-0.009) compared to the sample average.

Sentiment extracted from mentor comments seemed to to align with student sentiment differences from respective days; positive student sentiment differences were followed by positive mentor sentiments.

Sentiments and Hours Worked

The average sentiment difference was most positive in work days that were between 3 to 5 hours in length, with an average difference of -0.0125. This range also had the most logs, including the only hourly bins larger than 10,000 logs (n = 11,359 and 15,385 for 3 and 4 hours respectively). This implies that a strong portion of internships have work hours that maximize the student experience. On the other hand, a full-time working day, 8 hours, had the most negative sentiment differnece average.

Appendix

Sentiment Analysis Methodology

Two main techniques were utilized to ensure that we are getting the accurate values for the sentiment score associated with a piece of literature. The first technique involves the cleaning the data to only include valid check-in and checkout comments. Various NLP techniques such as lemmatizing are used to filter out strings that usually give inaccurate sentiment values such a string with only 2 or 3 words in them. After performing the different filtering techniques, the initial dataset was reduced from roughly 200K logs to around 60,000 records, approximately 30% of the starting dataset.

The second technique that was used was calculating the sentiment value for a piece of string using three different methods and averaging their results. The first method is to get sentiment value from the TextBlob NLP library, whose sentiment analysis tools are based off the Pattern library, which focuses on adjectives and was trained on customer review from popular online shopping platforms. The second method uses NTLK library’s Naïve Bayes Classifier which is a machine learning model trained on a large corpus of movie reviews. Finally, the last method utilizes VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary and Sentiment Reasoner) library which was trained on comments scrapped from various social media platforms. The average sentiment from these methods is normalized to map the value from 0.0 (most negative) to 1.0 (most positive).
